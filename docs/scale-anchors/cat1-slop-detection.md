# Cat 1: Slop Detection — Scale Anchors

Reference: check-inventory.md

> **Scoring floor**: The minimum score for any check is 5% (not 0%). This prevents a single zero from catastrophically collapsing the geometric mean. A score of 5% indicates actively hostile behavior — the absolute worst case. Reserve it for products that deliberately harm users in this dimension.

---

## 1.1 LLM Smell Markers in Text

**What it measures**: Frequency of detectable AI-generated writing patterns — banned words, em-dash density, structural tells, model-specific fingerprints.

| Score | Behavioral Description | Product Example |
|-------|----------------------|-----------------|
| 0% (5% floor) | Text is a raw LLM dump. Multiple banned words per paragraph, em-dash density above 1 per 200 words, Bold Prefix lists throughout, symmetrical paragraph structures, model-specific openers ("Certainly!", "Great question!"). No human editing visible. | AI-generated SEO content farms, drop-shipped product descriptions |
| 25% | Detectable LLM assistance with light editing. Banned-word frequency 3-6 per 1000 words, occasional em-dash clusters, some structural patterns (Rule of Three, symmetrical lists). A human touched it but didn't rewrite it. | Many "AI-powered" SaaS landing pages, corporate blogs using ChatGPT drafts |
| 50% | Moderate editing of AI-assisted content. Banned-word frequency 1-3 per 1000 words, em-dash density within normal range, structural patterns mostly broken up. Reads like a human who leans on AI for first drafts but rewrites substantially. | Well-maintained company blogs with AI writing assistance |
| 75% | Rare detectable markers. Banned-word frequency under 1 per 1000 words, no structural tells, voice is consistent and human. AI may have been used but the human's editing voice dominates completely. | Basecamp's Signal v. Noise blog, well-edited indie product copy |
| 100% | Zero detectable LLM markers. Writing has idiosyncratic voice, irregular rhythm, personal vocabulary. Could not have been generated by current models without extensive human rewriting that itself constitutes original writing. | Hand-written developer blogs (Julia Evans, Dan Luu), Craig Mod's essays |

**Scoring notes**: Use the banned-word list (49 words, 38 phrases, 16 openers) as the primary quantitative signal. Em-dash density above 1 per 500 words is a secondary flag. Structural patterns (Bold Prefix lists, Rule of Three, symmetrical paragraphs) are tertiary. Score on density, not binary presence — a single "delve" in 5000 words is noise, not signal.

## 1.2 Social Authenticity

**What it measures**: Whether engagement metrics, followers, and community participation reflect real human activity versus manufactured or bot-driven signals.

**N/A condition**: If product has no discoverable social media presence and no evidence of community engagement → N/A.

| Score | Behavioral Description | Product Example |
|-------|----------------------|-----------------|
| 0% (5% floor) | Community is visibly manufactured. Follower counts wildly disproportionate to engagement. Comment sections filled with generic praise ("Great post!", "So helpful!") from accounts with no history. Obvious bot networks amplifying content. Dead internet in human clothing. | Products with purchased followers, crypto projects with bot armies, engagement-farmed launch campaigns |
| 25% | Real humans exist but engagement is artificially inflated. Mix of genuine and bot accounts. Suspiciously uniform engagement patterns (all comments within 2 minutes of posting). Some authentic community buried under manufactured signals. | Twitter/X reply sections under promoted tweets, products using engagement pods |
| 50% | Community appears mostly real but shallow. Genuine followers but low meaningful interaction. Comments exist but are surface-level. No visible bot activity, but also no evidence the community actually uses or cares about the product. Passive audience, not active community. | Average SaaS with a real but disengaged Twitter following |
| 75% | Clearly authentic community with organic engagement patterns. Real humans discussing real use cases. Natural variation in comment timing, depth, and sentiment. Some criticism visible (a sign of health). Growth rate consistent with product quality. | Signal's user community, Tailwind CSS community discussions |
| 100% | Community is the product's immune system. Users file detailed bug reports, contribute improvements, argue about design decisions, create independent content about it. Growth is entirely organic. No engagement manipulation of any kind. The community would notice and revolt if metrics were faked. | Linux kernel mailing lists, Nostr protocol community, SQLite user ecosystem |

**Scoring notes**: Check follower-to-engagement ratios. Authentic accounts typically show 1-5% engagement rates. Uniform engagement timing (all likes within seconds) indicates coordination. Look for negative/critical comments — their absence in a large community is itself a red flag. A product with 500 genuine, active users scores higher than one with 50,000 bot followers.
