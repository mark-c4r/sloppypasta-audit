The Sloppypasta Audit

47 checks for sovereignty, craft, and honesty in internet products.

Two things broke the internet: no native money, and no taste. The first gave us surveillance advertising and subscription traps. The second gave us sloppypasta — AI-generated content nobody cared enough to stand behind. This audit checks both dimensions. The structural one: is this product built on protocols that can't rugpull you, or a platform that will? And the craft one: did someone actually give a damn about the words, the design, the experience — or did they ship a prompt and call it a product? Sovereignty without craft is ugly. Craft without sovereignty is a gilded cage.

Axioms

The worldview. If you disagree with these, the audit will produce results you find unfair. That's fine. Use a different audit.

Digital content has zero cost to copyPaywalls and DRM try to make information scarce. It isn't. Products should work with this property, not fight it — let people access first, pay voluntarily after.
Protocols can't de-platform you. Platforms can.A language doesn't require an account. HTTP doesn't need a login. When you use a protocol, no intermediary can silence you. That's the structural difference.
Crypto keys prove identity. Proof-of-work proves history.Without both, someone has to vouch for the data — and that someone becomes a single point of failure. Bitcoin showed that computational proof replaces trust.
Payments can't be faked the way engagement canViews, likes, followers, comments — all manufactured at scale for pennies. A sat payment requires actual economic commitment. That makes it a reliable signal.
People have multiple identities depending on contextYou use a different name at work, with friends, online. Forcing one legal identity onto all interactions enables surveillance and kills the freedom to be wrong publicly.
Pattern-matching is not the same as understandingLLMs produce statistically average output. That's useful for drafts and research. It's not useful for decisions that require taste, judgment, or caring about the outcome.
System architecture outlasts company policy"Don't be evil" depends on who's in charge. "Can't be evil" is enforced by code. If a system can betray its users — through terms changes, acquisitions, regulation — it eventually will.
The best products ship less, not moreOpinionated software that does 5 things well beats configurable software that does 50 things halfway. Saying no to features is the hardest and most valuable design skill.

The 47 Checks

Eight categories. Each heuristic scores on a 0–100% sliding scale. Most products land somewhere in the 20–80% range on most checks — a 0% means something is actively hostile or completely absent, which should be rare. The geometric mean scoring naturally makes low scores hurt more than high scores help, so you don't need to hand out zeros to make the math work. Let the scale do its job.

I. Content Integrity20%
Is this slop?
  • CI-0
    No access gates
    Paywalls, email-harvesting modals, "sign up to continue reading." If the content is worth reading, let people read it. Let them pay after, voluntarily.
  • CI-1
    No LLM smell
    Negation reframes ("It's not just X. It's Y."), sycophantic hedging, false authority, em-dash abuse, the unmistakable rhythm of a one-shot prompt.
  • CI-2
    Editorial judgment visible
    Someone chose what to include and what to cut. Curation, not aggregation. Selection, not completeness.
  • CI-3
    Someone stood behind it
    Named author. Iteration history. Corrections acknowledged. The opposite of content nobody signed.
  • CI-4
    Dense, not padded
    Signal per paragraph. No filler, no "related articles" spam, no engagement bait in the margins.
  • CI-5
    Provenance traceable
    Can you verify where this came from? Signed, timestamped, attributed to a source you can check.
  • CI-6
    No drive-by contributions
    If open-source: are AI-generated PRs from people who never read the codebase getting filtered out?
II. Sovereignty & Privacy20%
Can they rugpull you?
  • SP-1
    The rugpull test
    Can the operator shut you out, delete your data, change the terms, or sell you to an acquirer? If yes: not sovereign.
  • SP-2
    Exit cost near zero
    Can you leave and take everything with you? Data, content, identity, relationships — in a standard format you can use elsewhere.
  • SP-3
    No identity harvesting
    Ideal: keypair only, no account. Acceptable: minimal account, no email required. Failing: email-gated signup wall. Government ID or biometrics: always zero. "Legally required" is not an excuse — it's a reason to pick a different jurisdiction or become a cypherpunk.
  • SP-4
    No surveillance by default
    Tracking pixels, analytics scripts, third-party cookies, fingerprinting. What do they collect, and why?
  • SP-5
    Protocol, not platform
    Built on open protocols (Nostr, RSS, ActivityPub, SMTP) or locked to proprietary APIs?
  • SP-6
    You control the algorithm
    Can you see the ranking logic? Modify it? Turn it off? Or does a black box decide what you see?
  • SP-7
    Self-hostable or auditable
    Can you run your own instance? At minimum, can you read the source code?
  • SP-8
    Pay per service, not per month
    Ideal: sats in, service out, no relationship required. A subscription is honest when it's a bus ticket — bulk discount, simpler experience. A subscription is theft when the business model depends on people forgetting to cancel.
III. Craft & Care15%
Did someone give a damn?
  • CC-1
    Opinionated
    Does the product take a side? Or does it offer a settings page with 47 toggles because nobody could decide what to build?
  • CC-2
    Iteration visible
    Changelogs, blog posts about hard decisions, version history. Evidence that someone refined this, not just shipped it.
  • CC-3
    Words written, not generated
    Error messages, onboarding copy, the about page. Does it read like someone chose these words, or like a prompt chose them?
  • CC-4
    Less, on purpose
    Features that got cut. Scope that got reduced. The discipline of saying no to things users asked for because the product knows what it is.
  • CC-5
    No dark patterns
    Unsubscribe works. Cancellation is one click. No guilt modals. No "are you sure?" three times. No pre-checked boxes.
  • CC-6
    Respects your time
    You open it, get what you need, close it. Not engineered to keep you there. Async-first. No dopamine hooks.
IV. Openness10%
Does it play with others?
  • OI-1
    RSS or equivalent feed
    Syndication that works without an account. No "subscribe to our newsletter" as the only option.
  • OI-2
    API access
    Documented, stable, not rate-limited into uselessness.
  • OI-3
    Data portability
    Export everything. JSON, CSV, OPML, markdown. A format another tool can actually use.
  • OI-4
    Source available
    Can you read the code? Fork it? At minimum, can you see what's running?
  • OI-5
    Sovereign stack compatible
    Works with Nostr, Lightning, Bitcoin — or at least doesn't actively block them.
V. Honesty15%
Does it tell you the truth?
  • HT-1
    Business model visible
    How does this make money? If the answer isn't obvious within 30 seconds, you're the product.
  • HT-2
    No growth-hack language
    "AI-powered." "Revolutionary." "10x your productivity." If the marketing sounds like a LinkedIn post, the product was probably built like one too.
  • HT-3
    Honest about AI
    If AI generates or processes content, does the product say so? Or does it pretend a human wrote it?
  • HT-4
    No manufactured urgency
    Countdown timers. "Limited time." "X people viewing this now." The tricks of someone who doesn't trust their product to sell itself.
VI. Voice & Humanity10%
Is a human in there?
  • VH-1
    Distinctive voice
    Could you identify who made this from the writing alone? Or does it sound like every other SaaS landing page?
  • VH-2
    Humans visible
    Real names, real faces, a real about page. Not "our team of passionate innovators."
  • VH-3
    Lived experience
    The content reflects someone who's done the thing, not someone who researched the thing. Embodied knowledge, not aggregated information.
  • VH-4
    Dialogue, not broadcast
    Can you talk back? Reply, comment, respond? Or does content arrive and the conversation is over?
VII. Wisdom & Restraint5%
Does it know when to shut up?
  • WR-1
    Constraints are deliberate
    Limited by choice, not by accident. The product knows what it's not.
  • WR-2
    Can't nag you
    Ideal: holds no contact data — nagging is architecturally impossible. Acceptable: has your email, never uses it for re-engagement. Failing: "we miss you" campaigns. The test: is restraint enforced by design, or just by current policy?
  • WR-3
    Decades, not quarters
    Will this still work in 10 years? Or does it depend on a VC runway, a trending API, or a hype cycle?
  • WR-4
    AI builds understanding
    If the product uses AI: does it help you learn, or does it replace your thinking? One makes you smarter. The other makes you dependent.
VIII. Value Alignment5%
Who captures the value?
  • VA-1
    Value reaches creators
    Does the person who made the content get paid? Or does the platform take it?
  • VA-2
    Your attention isn't the product
    Designed to help you finish and leave, or to keep you scrolling?
  • VA-3
    Honest exchange
    Ideal: V4V or pay-per-use. A subscription is fine when it's a bus ticket — bulk discount, simpler UX. A subscription is theft when the business depends on people forgetting to cancel. The test: would they make the same money if every customer actively chose each month?
  • VA-4
    Curators get value
    Does the system reward people who find and surface good content? Or only the people who create it?

Scoring

The formula is a geometric mean — multiply, don't add. This has one property that matters: low scores hurt disproportionately. A 15% on one category doesn't just lose you a few points — it drags the entire product score down through multiplication. You can't compensate for a structural failure with nice fonts. The math does what the philosophy demands.

How it works

Each heuristic scores 0–100% on a sliding scale. Score what you actually observe — most things aren't binary.

Scoring guide

RangeWhat it meansWhen to use
85–100%ExemplaryThis is what the ideal looks like. No meaningful gaps.
60–84%Good with gapsThe intent is clear. Execution has room to improve.
30–59%MixedSome effort visible, but significant problems undermine it.
10–29%PoorThe product is working against this principle. Maybe unintentionally, but the result is the same.
1–9%HostileActively harmful on this dimension. Rare — most "terrible" things are still a 10%.
0%Absent or antagonisticReserved for the worst cases: eyeball scanning, complete fraud, total surveillance. Should be rare. If you're handing out zeros often, you're scoring too harshly.

Category score = geometric mean of its heuristic scores.
Overall score = weighted geometric mean of category scores.
N/A heuristics are excluded from the mean.

-- Category score (geometric mean of heuristics)
category = (h1 × h2 × h3 × ... × hN) ^ (1/N)

-- Overall score (weighted geometric mean of categories)
overall = cat1^w1 × cat2^w2 × ... × cat8^w8

-- In Excel:
category  = EXP(AVERAGE(LN(scores/100))) * 100
overall   = EXP(SUMPRODUCT(weights, LN(cats/100))) * 100

Why geometric, not arithmetic

Arithmetic mean: add and divide. A 15% in Sovereignty with 20% weight just costs you ~17 points. Score well elsewhere and you're a C. A scammer with good design passes the test.

Geometric mean: multiply. A 15% in Sovereignty drags the entire product down through the multiplication chain. You can't add your way out of a structural failure. The lower the score, the louder it pulls.

The FTX test. A product scoring 95% Craft, 90% Voice, 85% Content — but 15% Sovereignty (demands biometrics, no exit) and 10% Honesty (hidden business model). Arithmetic average: 59%, a C. Geometric mean: 43%, a D. And if Sovereignty actually hits 0% (literal eyeball scanning): overall goes to 0%, an F. Beautiful typography doesn't save fraud. FTX had great design. Sam had a nice hoodie.

Note the difference: a product scoring 15% on Sovereignty gets a D — bad, but still on the scale. A product at 0% gets an F — off the scale. That 15-point gap between "terrible" and "absent" is meaningful. The sliding scale lets you make that distinction. A binary pass/fail can't.
