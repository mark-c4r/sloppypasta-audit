## Audit Scope

**Surfaces assessed**: thefrontier.fm homepage (direct fetch); front page, feed page, article detail pages; RSS feeds /rss/3day and /rss/7day (28 items confirmed); source code (External + Source mode — layout.tsx, digest.ts, V4VModal, feedback-widget.js, DESIGN.md directly read); filter/empty state UX; V4V payment flow (examined in source); accessibility (focus-visible, aria-label counts via source grep).
**Surfaces via proxy**: Social engagement on Twitter @thefrontierfm and Nostr npub from observable counts — not firsthand interaction. Business model sustainability inferred from V4V presence and code inspection rather than confirmed revenue.
**Surfaces not assessed**: No mobile app exists (6.9, 6.10, 6.11 N/A). Private GitHub repo (no external access to git history or CI). Admin dashboard/content pipeline backend. Live Lightning payment execution.
**Limitations**: Private repo means git history and CI are not independently verifiable. No mobile app means [if:mobile] checks N/A, affecting Cat 5/6 weight redistribution.

---

## Overall: 63% -- Grade C

The Frontier is a genuinely well-crafted content product with strong product conduct (87%), excellent economic alignment (88%), and a distinctive design system (77%). Its newspaper-aesthetic layout, absence of dark patterns, Lightning-based V4V payments, and full-content RSS feeds demonstrate real care and opinionated product thinking. The product is dragged down significantly by sovereignty and privacy concerns (42%) -- primarily a closed-source private repository with no self-hosting option, creating platform dependency. Content provenance (AI-generated summaries from podcast transcripts) lacks transparent labeling at the point of encounter. Social presence is nascent, and the business model is not yet clearly visible to visitors.

## Priority Improvements

1. **Open Source the Codebase (6.7: 10 -> 75, 6.5: 25 -> 75)**: The single most impactful change. Publishing the source code under MIT/AGPL would lift Cat 6 from 42% to approximately 60%, and the overall score from 63% to approximately 70%. The product's architecture (Next.js + SQLite + LLM pipeline) has no competitive moat from secrecy -- the curation pipeline and show list ARE the moat. Open-sourcing would also enable self-hosting (6.5) and improve rugpull resistance (6.1).

2. **Add AI Disclosure at Point of Encounter (7.3: 65 -> 85)**: Add a visible label or methodology note on each article indicating AI involvement ("Cross-referenced and summarized by AI from podcast transcripts"). The footer already says "compiled from X shows, Y episodes analyzed" -- making the AI role explicit is a small copy change with significant honesty impact. Consider an /about or /methodology page explaining the pipeline.

3. **Make Business Model Visible (7.1: 45 -> 75)**: Add an "About" or "How This Works" page explaining V4V, the podcast curation model, and how the product sustains itself. Currently a visitor unfamiliar with Lightning/V4V has no way to understand the value exchange. A single page with "We make money when you voluntarily send sats to podcast creators" would suffice.

4. **Sign Content with Nostr Events (6.8: 35 -> 80)**: The Nostr infrastructure is already in place (NIP-05, nostr-tools dependency, npub). Publishing editions as signed Nostr events would provide cryptographic content provenance, lifting 6.8 dramatically and contributing to protocol-vs-platform (6.4) scoring.

5. **Add Public Changelog or Development Blog (4.4: 55 -> 75, 7.5: 40 -> 60)**: The product has active development (30+ commits/month, extensive decisions log) but none of this is visible to users. A /changelog page auto-generated from git history, or periodic Nostr notes about development, would demonstrate iteration and create a dialogue channel simultaneously.
